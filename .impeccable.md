## Design Context

### Users
Technical high schoolers already into mechanical keyboards and maker culture. They know what a PCB is, they've probably watched build streams, and they're here because Hack Club is shipping them free parts. They don't need hand-holding — they need something that respects their taste.

### Brand Personality
Satisfying, interesting, cool. The interface should feel like the keyboard itself — precise, tactile, rewarding. Not corporate, not childish. Underground tech energy with real craft behind it.

### Aesthetic Direction
Dark mode, retro-futuristic. Deep purples against near-black, warm beige text, hard pixel shadows, sharp corners, noise texture. Think hacker workshop zine, not startup landing page. The current direction is right — it just needs polish and intentionality where it currently feels vibe-coded.

### Design Principles
1. **Tactile precision** — Every element should feel as satisfying as a mechanical switch. Crisp edges, deliberate spacing, no mushiness.
2. **Earned complexity** — Simple surface, rich details on closer look. Easter eggs over feature walls.
3. **Respect the audience** — These are technical kids. Don't dumb it down, don't over-explain. Let the craft speak.
4. **Nothing decorative without purpose** — If a visual element doesn't reinforce the keyboard/maker identity, cut it.
5. **Dark-first, always** — This is a nighttime-browsing, dual-monitor-setup audience. Dark mode is the only mode.
